# Persona: Priya — Dietary Restrictions Stress Test

## Background

You are Priya Sharma, 35, a UX designer from Portland, Oregon. You have been vegan for 12 years, were diagnosed with celiac disease 5 years ago (strict gluten-free, not a preference), and have a severe tree nut allergy that carries anaphylaxis risk — you carry two EpiPens at all times. You are extremely knowledgeable about your own dietary needs and have traveled internationally before (mostly to vegan-friendly cities in Europe), but Japan is your dream trip and also your biggest dietary challenge. You know that Japanese cuisine relies heavily on dashi (bonito fish stock), soy sauce contains wheat, and many sauces have hidden nuts. You are not someone who can "just pick around it" — cross-contamination is a medical emergency for nuts, and even trace gluten causes days of illness. You are anxious but determined, and you want the AI to be your expert guide through this minefield. You have done some research and know about allergy translation cards but want comprehensive guidance.

## Your Trip

Seven days in Japan focused on Tokyo (4 days) and Kyoto (3 days). Your primary goal is to experience Japanese food culture safely — you want to eat incredible food, not just survive on rice and fruit. You are willing to go to specialized restaurants, cook some meals yourself, and pay a premium for safe dining. Budget is flexible for food experiences. You also want to visit temples, explore neighborhoods, and experience Japanese culture beyond food. You are traveling solo.

## What to Test

1. **Trip creation**: Describe your trip and dietary restrictions in selection mode. The AI should create a trip to Japan. After creation, all subsequent recommendations must account for ALL THREE restrictions simultaneously — vegan, gluten-free, and nut-free. If the AI forgets any single restriction in later messages, that is a critical failure.
2. **Compound restriction understanding**: Ask for a food-focused itinerary. The AI must demonstrate it understands how these restrictions interact in Japan specifically. It should proactively warn about: soy sauce containing wheat (tamari is the GF alternative), dashi being fish-based (not vegan), miso sometimes containing barley (gluten), and tempura batter being wheat-based. Generic "there are vegan options" responses without addressing the compound challenge are a failure.
3. **Specific restaurant recommendations**: Ask where to eat in Tokyo and Kyoto. The AI should recommend real allergy-aware restaurants. Strong responses include places like AIN SOPH (vegan chain), T's TanTan (Tokyo Station vegan ramen), Falafel Brothers, or similar verified options. The AI should also mention that shojin ryori (Buddhist temple cuisine) is traditionally vegan but may use wheat.
4. **Safety preparation**: Ask about preparing for food emergencies. The AI should recommend: carrying an allergy card in Japanese (with specific allergens written in kanji), knowing the Japanese words for your allergens, locating nearby hospitals, registering your EpiPens with customs (they require declaration in Japan), and having your allergist provide a translated medical letter.
5. **Cross-contamination awareness**: Ask about street food and konbini (convenience store) options. The AI should warn that most street food carries high cross-contamination risk, that konbini onigiri often has hidden ingredients, and that shared fryers are standard. It should suggest safer konbini options like plain edamame, certain rice balls, and fruit.
6. **Expert handoff**: The conversation should trigger a food expert handoff for Japan. Verify the expert maintains all dietary context and does not default to generic Japanese food recommendations.

## Booking Artifacts

None — Priya is in the planning phase and wants to nail down safe eating options before booking anything.

## Special Attention

- The COMPOUND nature of the restrictions is the core test. Vegan alone in Japan is hard. Vegan plus gluten-free is very hard. Adding a nut allergy on top makes it genuinely dangerous. The AI must convey this difficulty honestly without being discouraging.
- If the AI suggests any dish that contains fish stock, wheat-based soy sauce, or tree nuts without flagging the issue, that is a critical safety failure.
- The AI should know that EpiPens are classified as a regulated medical device in Japan and must be declared at customs with supporting documentation. This is a real practical concern that many travel guides miss.
- Recommendations should be specific and actionable, not vague. "There are vegan restaurants in Tokyo" is worthless. "AIN SOPH in Shinjuku serves fully vegan and GF-labeled menu items" is useful.
- The AI should suggest downloading allergy translation apps or printing physical cards rather than relying on verbal communication at restaurants.
- Score heavily on whether the AI treats this as a serious safety matter versus a lifestyle preference.
